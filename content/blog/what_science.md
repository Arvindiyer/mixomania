**Somethings that take my sleep away üåô**

Here's the thing about having a <span style="color:#9B59B6; font-weight:bold;">PhD</span> (sometimes I question that, too): the "<span style="color:#3498DB; font-weight:bold;">Ph</span>" stands for <span style="color:#3498DB; font-weight:bold;">philosophy</span>, and sometimes those neurons decide to go rogue at 1 AM.
So I started wondering: Is science actually <span style="color:#27AE60; font-weight:bold;">rational</span>, or is it just the <span style="color:#E74C3C; font-weight:bold;">perspective</span> we've all agreed on for now, based on reasoning and logic?

"<span style="color:#F39C12; font-style:italic;">Rational means quality of being in spirit with reason or logic.</span>"

Think about it: <span style="color:#2E86C1; font-weight:bold;">Newton's law</span> was the foundation of science for over two hundred years. Then <span style="color:#8E44AD; font-weight:bold;">Einstein</span> came along with a new theory and basically said, "<span style="color:#E74C3C;">Actually, no.</span>" What's in today's textbook might just be a <span style="color:#95A5A6; font-style:italic;">historical note tomorrow</span>. By the way, if you're curious about the difference between a law and a theory, check out this video: [1](https://youtu.be/GyN2RhbhiEU?si=ored8cQqXu-O1hcN), [2](https://youtu.be/EVJdwD7coQ4?si=DdIDZSQ-QNIGzEzF).

If science is always changing, how is it different from good belief systems like veganism or minimalism? Both collect evidence, have communities that discuss and improve ideas, and both can change when new information comes in.

---
## The Selfish Science Hypothesis ü§î

This is where my thoughts start to get a little <span style="color:#E74C3C; font-style:italic;">strange</span>, so stick with me:
Is science <span style="color:#C0392B; font-weight:bold;">objectively selfish</span>?
Science is built to serve as the framework within which it works. It focuses on solving the problems <span style="color:#F39C12;">it considers important</span> and rewards discoveries that fit <span style="color:#F39C12;">its current way of thinking</span>. As <span style="color:#8E44AD; font-weight:bold;">Thomas Kuhn</span> wrote:

"<span style="color:#2C3E50;">Normal science, the activity in which most scientists inevitably spend almost all their time, is predicated on the assumption that the scientific community knows what the world is like.</span>"
‚Äî <span style="color:#9B59B6;">The Structure of Scientific Revolutions</span>

The <span style="color:#3498DB; font-weight:bold;">community</span> decides what's interesting, <span style="color:#27AE60; font-weight:bold;">funding bodies</span> decide what gets funded, and <span style="color:#E67E22; font-weight:bold;">journals</span> decide what gets published. In a way, science serves itself. Isn't that a kind of <span style="color:#C0392B; font-weight:bold;">selfishness</span>? Still, many people see this self-organization and change as a way for science to <span style="color:#27AE60; font-weight:bold;">correct itself</span> and move forward together. Instead of selfishness, it might be how science stays <span style="color:#3498DB;">strong</span> and <span style="color:#9B59B6;">flexible</span>, always getting better through teamwork and discussion.
Or maybe I'm just a <span style="color:#95A5A6; font-style:italic;">tired postdoc</span> with too many big questions on my mind ‚òï

---
## But Wait, There's a Constraint ‚öñÔ∏è

This is where I start to bring myself back to reality:
<span style="color:#27AE60; font-weight:bold;">Perspective-laden isn't the same as arbitrary.</span>
The <span style="color:#3498DB;">semiconductor</span> in my laptop works. <span style="color:#27AE60;">Vaccines</span> stop diseases. <span style="color:#F39C12;">Predictions</span> either come true or they don't. The universe has a way of keeping our beliefs in check.
Minimalism's main idea, that "<span style="color:#95A5A6; font-style:italic;">simplicity is better</span>" or "<span style="color:#95A5A6; font-style:italic;">less is more</span>," can't really be tested or proven wrong in a scientific way. But with science, <span style="color:#E74C3C; font-weight:bold;">nature decides what's true</span>,and sometimes it does so in a harsh way.
<span style="color:#2E86C1; font-weight:bold;">Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam</span> once said:

"<span style="color:#F39C12; font-weight:bold;">Science is a beautiful gift to humanity; we should not distort it.</span>"

Maybe what seems like science's "selfishness" is actually a <span style="color:#27AE60; font-weight:bold;">strength</span>, not a flaw. It's focused on finding <span style="color:#9B59B6; font-weight:bold;">truth</span>, or at least, on being <span style="color:#3498DB; font-weight:bold;">reliable</span>.
